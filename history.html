History of Abisko National Park



Abisko National Park has a rich history as both a cultural and scientific landmark. For thousands of years, the Sami people lived in the area, following herds of wild reindeer between the woodlands and mountains. Evidence of their traditional way of life, such as ancient pitfalls for hunting and traces of old Sami huts, can still be seen today. Over time, the Sami transitioned to reindeer herding, a practice that continues through the Gabna Sami reindeer herding district, with many members now living in the nearby Rensjön community. Today, the Sami still use the land for reindeer herding, while visitors enjoy the park for recreation, and scientists conduct research (Abisko National Park: History, Sveriges Nationalparker).

Established in 1909, Abisko National Park was one of Sweden's first national parks. It was created by the Swedish government, driven by scientists like geologist Fredrik Svenonius, to preserve the unique northern Nordic landscape in its original condition and to provide a space for scientific research and public appreciation. The park's accessibility improved significantly with the construction of the railway from Kiruna to Narvik, which brought both workers and tourists to the area. The historic "Rallarvägen" trail, once used for transporting railway materials, is now a popular hiking path (Abisko National Park: Park Protection, National Parks and Abisko National Park: History, Wikipedia).

Today, Abisko serves as a hub for scientific study at the Abisko Scientific Research Station, where researchers from around the world investigate Arctic climate change and other environmental topics. The park also draws visitors for its exceptional aurora borealis viewing, thanks to its clear skies and favorable location in the Atlantic's rain shadow. Its pristine environment and rich cultural history make it a unique destination for both study and recreation .
