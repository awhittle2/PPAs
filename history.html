Abisko National Park History


Abisko National Park has a rich history as both a cultural and scientific landmark. For thousands of years, the Sami people lived in the area, following herds of wild reindeer between the woodlands and mountains. Evidence of their traditional way of life, such as ancient pitfalls for hunting and traces of old Sami huts, can still be seen today. Over time, the Same transitioned to reindeer herding, a practice that continues through the Gabna Sami reindeer herding district, with many members now living in the nearby Rensjon community.

Established in 1909, Abisko National Park was one of Sweden's first national parks, created to preserve the unique northern Nordic landscape and to support scientific research. The park's accessibility improved significantly with the contruction of the railway from Kiruna to Narvik, which brought both workers and tourists to the area. The historic "Rallarvagen" trail, once used for transporting railway materials, is now a popular hiking path.

Today, Abisko is a hub for scientific study at the Abisko Scientific Research Station, where researchers from around the world investigate Artice climate change and other environmental topics. The park also draws visitors for its exceptional aurora borealis viewing, thanks for its clear skies and favorable location in the Atlantic's rain shadow.
